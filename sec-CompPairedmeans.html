    Subsection 7.2.1 Understanding the Basics

    The paired \(t\)-test for means is used to compare two related measurements to determine whether their population mean difference is significantly different from zero. This test is appropriate when the data come in natural pairs, such as before-and-after measurements on the same individuals or matched observations from closely related subjects. Instead of comparing two independent sample means, the paired \(t\)-test analyzes the differences within each pair. If we let \(d\) represent the difference for each pair, then the hypotheses are written in terms of the population mean difference \(\mu_d\text{.}\) The null hypothesis is \(\mu_d = 0\) and the alternative hypothesis can be either \(\mu_d \lt 0\) or \(\mu_d \gt 0 \) for a one tailed test, or \(\mu_d \ne 0\) for a two-tailed test .
    🔗
    The test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} t=\frac{\bar{d}-\mu_d}{s_d/\sqrt{n}} \end{equation*}
    where \(\bar{d}\) is the sample mean of the differences, \(s_d\) is the sample standard deviation of the differences, \(n\) is the number of pairs in the sample, and \(\mu_{d}\) is the hypothesized mean difference, usually \(0\) indicating no difference in values.
    🔗
    This test assumes that the data consist of matched pairs, that the differences are independent from one pair to another, and that the distribution of the differences is approximately normal, especially when the sample size is small. The resulting p-value is used to decide whether there is enough evidence to conclude that a true mean difference exists between the paired observations.
    🔗
    Recall that when the p-value \(\lt \alpha \) we reject the null hypothesis, otherwise there is not enough evidence to reject the null hypothesis.
    🔗

    Definition 7.2.1.

    A paired \(t\)-test for the mean is a statistical test used to compare the mean of the differences between two related measurements and determine whether there is evidence that the population mean difference is different from a hypothesized value.
    🔗
    The test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} t=\frac{\bar{d}-\mu_d}{s_d/\sqrt{n}} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    where \(\bar{d}\) is the sample mean of the differences, \(s_d\) is the sample standard deviation of the differences, \(n\) is the number of pairs, and \(\mu_d\) is the hypothesized population mean difference.
    🔗
    🔗
    A confidence interval for a paired mean is used when data are collected in matched pairs, such as before-and-after measurements on the same subjects or measurements on two related conditions. First, compute the difference for each pair, usually written as \(d = x_1 - x_2\) or in a context-specific way such as \(d=\text{After}-\text{Before}\text{.}\) Then treat the list of differences as a single sample. If the conditions for inference are satisfied, a confidence interval for the population mean difference \(\mu_d\) is given by \(\bar{d}\pm t^*\dfrac{s_d}{\sqrt{n}}\text{,}\) where \(\bar{d}\) is the sample mean of the differences, \(s_d\) is the sample standard deviation of the differences, \(n\) is the number of pairs, and \(t^*\) is the critical value from the \(t\)-distribution with \(n-1\) degrees of freedom.
    🔗

    Definition 7.2.2.

    A confidence interval for paired means is used to estimate the population mean difference \(\mu_d\) when data are collected in matched pairs. The confidence interval for the population mean difference is
    \begin{equation*} \bar{d} \pm t^*\dfrac{s_d}{\sqrt{n}}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(\bar{d}\) is the sample mean of the differences, \(s_d\) is the sample standard deviation of the differences, \(n\) is the number of pairs, and \(t^*\) is the critical value from the \(t\)-distribution with \(n-1\) degrees of freedom.
    🔗

    We test \(H_0: \mu_d = 0\) versus \(H_a: \mu_d \gt 0\text{,}\) where \(\mu_d\) is the mean difference in push-ups. Since the number of push ups increased we are testing if it is greater than 0, thus a right tailed test.

    Solution 3. Statistical Test
    The sample mean of the differences is \(\bar{d} = \frac{14}{5} = 2.8\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The sample standard deviation of the differences is \(s_d \approx 0.447\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The test statistic is \(t = \frac{\bar{d}-0}{s_d/\sqrt{n}} = \frac{2.8}{0.447/\sqrt{5}} \approx 14.0\) with \(df = 5-1 = 4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    This gives a very small \(p\)-value \(\approx 0.000076\text{,}\) so we reject \(H_0\) since it is less than \(\alpha = .05\text{.}\)

    This gives a very small \(p\)-value \(\approx 0.000076\text{,}\) so we reject \(H_0\) since it is less than \(\alpha = .05\text{.}\)

    There is strong evidence that the 6-week training program increases the mean number of push-ups participants can complete in one minute.

    Example 7.2.5. Pushup Confidence Interval.

    To construct a 95% confidence interval for the mean difference in the number of pushups after the training program, let \(d=\text{After}-\text{Before}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution 1. Find t*
    Since the population standard deviation is unknown and the data are paired, we use a \(t\)-distribution with \(df=5-1=4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    With StatKey, under the Theoretical Distributions select t, enter the degrees of freedom of \(4\text{,}\) click onTwo Tail and enter 0.95 in the center area region. The \(t^* \) value is along the bottom of the graph for \(t^* =2.776\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Interval
    The differences between the paired values are \(3, 3, 3, 2, 3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    From these data, \(n=5\text{,}\) \(\bar{d}= 2.8\text{,}\) And \(s_d\approx 0.447\text{.}\)
    🔗
    For a \(95\%\) confidence interval with \(df=4\text{,}\) the critical value Is \(t^*\approx 2.776\) using StatKey.
    🔗
    The margin of error is
    \begin{equation*} ME=t^*\frac{s_d}{\sqrt{n}} =2.776\left(\frac{0.447}{\sqrt{5}}\right) \approx 0.555\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The confidence interval is
    \begin{equation*} \bar{d}\pm ME = 2.8 \pm 0.555\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    So the \(95\%\) confidence interval for the mean difference is \((2.245,\ 3.355)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We are 95% confident that the true mean difference in the number of pushups after the training program is between \(2.2\) and \(3.4\) more pushups. Since the interval does not contain 0, this is consistent with the hypothesis test that the training program increases the mean number of pushups.
